Overview

Project Overview

When retailers open new stores, a critical question emerges: Is the new location generating genuine business growth, or simply redistributing existing customers?

Business Problem

Store cannibalization occurs when a new retail location reduces sales from existing stores rather than generating incremental revenue. Understanding this effect is crucial for expansion strategy and resource allocation.

Analytical Approach

By examining customer transaction patterns before and after new store openings, the analysis categorizes members based on behavioral shifts and quantifies their impact on existing store performance.

Key Insights

The framework decomposes cannibalization into distinct components—retained, recalled, activated, and newbuy members—each revealing different aspects of customer response to new store availability.

Customer Segmentation

Member Behavior Categories

Customers are segmented into five distinct categories based on their purchase patterns before and after new store openings

Active Members

All customers who made purchases during the analysis period, serving as the baseline population for behavioral analysis.

Retained Members

Customers who remained active in both the previous and current periods, representing the stable customer base whose spending patterns may shift.

Activated Members

Previously dormant customers (≥12 months inactive) who resumed purchasing after the new store opening, indicating reactivation potential.

Recalled Members

Customers who returned after extended absence, similar to activated members but with distinct behavioral characteristics and spending patterns.

Newbuy Members

First-time customers in the current period, representing potential market expansion rather than redistribution of existing business.

Measurement

Cannibalization Metrics

The framework calculates four component metrics that together quantify the total cannibalization effect. Positive values indicate business growth; negative values indicate business diversion from existing stores.

Retained Cannibalization

Measures how existing customers redistribute their spending between existing and new stores when the new store becomes available, capturing the core redistribution effect.

Recalled Cannibalization

Quantifies the impact of returned customers choosing between existing and new stores, revealing preference shifts among reactivated members.

Newbuy Cannibalization

Assesses whether new customers are genuinely incremental or would have purchased at existing stores regardless of new location availability.

Activated Cannibalization

Evaluates the net effect of previously dormant customers becoming active, distinguishing between market expansion and store switching.

Architecture

Technical Architecture

A modular architecture separating data extraction, processing, and calculation concerns for maintainability and scalability

SQL Query Layer

14 parameterized queries for member behavior extraction with city-level and store-level analysis variants, turnover calculation queries by member type, and date-based period definitions.

Processing Module

Automated data pipeline orchestration with CLI arguments for city and step selection, automatic file management and versioning, error handling and logging.

Calculation Engine

Pivot table-based data aggregation, mathematical formula implementation, multi-city batch processing, and CSV output generation.

Analysis Notebooks

Exploratory data analysis, methodology development, visualization and validation, and documentation of calculation logic.

Key Finding

Distance Effect

Statistical analysis reveals that distance is the most significant factor influencing store cannibalization, following an exponential decay pattern

Exponential Decay Relationship

Regression analysis of cannibalization data across multiple cities shows a strong negative correlation between distance and cannibalization intensity:

  • Nearby stores experience the most severe cannibalization effects, with customer redistribution being most pronounced.
  • Intermediate distances show moderate cannibalization, where the new store captures some existing customers but also attracts new market segments.
  • Distant stores experience minimal to no cannibalization, operating in effectively independent markets.

The statistical model confirms distance as the dominant predictor, explaining a significant proportion of the variance in cannibalization effects across store pairs.

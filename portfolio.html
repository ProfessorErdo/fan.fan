The Problem

The Complexity of Range Planning

Retail assortment planning is a high-stakes decision affecting revenue, inventory costs, and customer satisfaction. Traditional approaches rely on manual analysis, historical intuition, and one-size-fits-all templates that fail to capture store-specific dynamics.

The challenge is compounded by seasonal variations (different ranges for Spring/Summer vs. Autumn/Winter), space constraints (linear meter limits), and the need to balance iconic products with local preferences.

  • ✕ Manual range selection lacks quantitative rigor
  • ✕ Different stores have different customer bases
  • ✕ Seasonal variations require separate planning
  • ✕ Space constraints (linear meters) must be respected
  • ✕ Compulsory products must be included regardless of performance

Pareto Principle in Retail

The Problem

The Limitation of Traditional PLC Frameworks

The Product Life Cycle (PLC) framework is a cornerstone of strategic marketing and product management. However, its practical utility is severely limited by a critical gap: the inability to accurately predict when products transition between stages.

Traditional approaches rely on manual analysis, historical analogies, or simple heuristics—methods that fail to capture the complex patterns in modern retail data.

  • ✕ Subjective assessments lead to inconsistent predictions
  • ✕ Lack of quantitative metrics for lifecycle stage transitions
  • ✕ Inability to process large-scale turnover data efficiently
  • ✕ No systematic way to identify seasonal vs. structural patterns
  • ✕ Reactive rather than proactive decision-making

Data Pipeline

Seven-stage processing pipeline from raw data to churn predictions

Step 0: Data Extraction

Query member data from CNDP warehouse including RFM metrics, engagement, demographics, and churn labels

Step 1: Data Preprocessing

Split by frequency (≤2 vs. >2 purchases), filter invalid records (negative values, empty age groups), handle outliers

Step 2: Feature Engineering

Create derived features: recency_minus_freq, lifespan_times_monetary, top1_sports_spending_percentage, lifespan_devidedby_tenure

Step 3: Customer Segmentation

Transform skewed data (PowerTransformer), scale features (MinMaxScaler), find optimal K (KneeLocator), apply K-means clustering

Step 4: Feature Selection

RFECV with LightGBM, 5-fold stratified CV, precision scoring, select top features that maximize predictive power

Hyperopt Bayesian optimization (200 trials), Tomek Links under-sampling, 5-fold CV, save best model to OSS

Step 6: Churn Prediction

Load trained model, generate churn probabilities for current members, rank by risk, export to CSV for intervention

The Problem

The Limitation of Traditional Segmentation

Traditional customer segmentation relies on demographics (age, gender, location) or simple RFM (Recency, Frequency, Monetary) analysis. These approaches fail to capture the rich behavioral patterns in modern transaction data.

Customers who look similar demographically may have completely different purchase behaviors. Conversely, customers from different demographics may share similar shopping patterns. Understanding true customer segments requires analyzing actual purchase behavior at the transaction level.

  • ✕ Demographics don't predict purchase behavior
  • ✕ Manual segmentation is subjective and inconsistent
  • ✕ Difficulty handling high-dimensional behavioral data
  • ✕ No systematic way to determine optimal segment count
  • ✕ Segments become outdated without regular refresh
